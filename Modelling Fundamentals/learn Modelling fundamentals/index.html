    Financial Modelling Fundamentals

    Turning Business Stories into Numbers

    Financial modelling is one of the most valuable skills in modern finance.

    At its core, a financial model is simply a structured representation of a business built in Excel (or another spreadsheet tool). It helps finance professionals understand how a company operates today and how it may perform in the future.

    Whether you're valuing a company, evaluating an acquisition, preparing a budget, or analysing an investment, financial modelling provides a framework for making informed decisions.

    Why Financial Modelling Matters

    Finance professionals build models to answer questions such as:

    • How much is a company worth?
    • What happens if revenue grows by 10%?
    • Can the business support additional debt?
    • Should the company acquire a competitor?
    • How sensitive are profits to changes in costs?
    • What return might investors expect?

    Rather than relying on intuition alone, financial models allow decisions to be tested using data and assumptions.

    What Is a Financial Model?

    A financial model combines:

    • Historical financial information
    • Business assumptions
    • Mathematical relationships
    • Financial logic

    to estimate future business performance.

    Most models forecast:

    • Revenue
    • Costs
    • Profitability
    • Cash Flow
    • Balance Sheet
    • Financing
    • Company valuation

    A model is only as good as the assumptions that drive it.

    The Building Blocks of Every Model

    Most financial models follow a similar structure.

    Inputs

    These are assumptions controlled by the modeller.

    Examples include:

    • Revenue growth
    • Gross margin
    • Tax rate
    • Interest rate
    • Capital expenditure
    • Working capital assumptions

    Calculations

    This is where business logic is applied.

    Examples:

    • Revenue forecasts
    • Depreciation schedules
    • Debt schedules
    • Interest calculations
    • Cash flow projections

    Outputs

    Outputs communicate the results of the model.

    Common outputs include:

    • Income Statement
    • Balance Sheet
    • Cash Flow Statement
    • Valuation
    • Charts
    • Dashboards
    • Scenario comparisons

    Types of Financial Models

    Different business questions require different models.

    Three-Statement Model

    The foundation of corporate finance.

    Links together:

    • Income Statement
    • Balance Sheet
    • Cash Flow Statement

    Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)

    Used to estimate the intrinsic value of a business based on expected future cash flows.

    Commonly used in:

    • Investment Banking
    • Equity Research
    • Private Equity
    • Corporate Development

    Leveraged Buyout (LBO)

    Evaluates whether an acquisition financed with debt can generate attractive investor returns.

    Typically used by:

    • Private Equity firms

    Merger Model

    Assesses the financial impact of combining two companies.

    Often referred to as:

    • Accretion/Dilution Analysis

    Used in:

    • Mergers & Acquisitions
    • Corporate Development

    Characteristics of a Good Financial Model

    Professional financial models should be:

    Transparent

    Users should easily understand the logic.

    Flexible

    Assumptions should be easy to modify.

    Accurate

    Calculations should be reliable and internally consistent.

    Auditable

    Errors should be easy to identify.

    Well Formatted

    Professional formatting improves readability and reduces mistakes.

    Common Modelling Best Practices

    Experienced financial modellers typically follow several principles:

    • Separate inputs, calculations, and outputs.
    • Avoid hardcoding values inside formulas.
    • Use consistent formatting.
    • Build models in logical sections.
    • Include error checks.
    • Document key assumptions.
    • Keep formulas as simple as possible.

    Good models are designed to be maintained—not just built.

    Where AI Fits into Financial Modelling

    Artificial Intelligence is changing how finance professionals build and review models.

    AI can assist with:

    • Formula generation
    • Error detection
    • Documentation
    • Data extraction
    • Financial statement analysis
    • Spreadsheet automation

    However, AI cannot replace:

    • Business judgement
    • Financial reasoning
    • Assumption selection
    • Understanding company strategy

    The strongest finance professionals will use AI as a productivity tool rather than a substitute for financial expertise.
