# AI Walkthrough - Financial Model Opinion Analysis

Imagine you have just joined an Investment Banking team.

Your Senior Associate has handed you Breville's FY2025 Annual Report and given you your first assignment.

> **Build a financial model that accurately represents how Breville operates as a business.**

Before opening Excel, answer the question every analyst should ask:

> **What are the economic drivers of this business?**

A financial model is not a collection of formulas.

It is a representation of how a company creates value.

---

## Step 1 — Understand the Business

Begin by reading the Annual Report from start to finish.

Focus on understanding the business before thinking about numbers.

| Question | Breville Observation | Why It Matters |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| What does Breville sell? | Premium small domestic kitchen appliances including coffee machines, ovens, blenders and food preparation products. | Understanding the products explains how revenue is generated. |
| Who are Breville's customers? | Consumers seeking premium kitchen appliances across global markets. | Customer demographics influence pricing power and long-term demand. |
| Where does Breville operate? | Australia, New Zealand, North America, Europe and selected international markets. | Geographic diversification affects both growth opportunities and risk. |
| How does Breville compete? | Innovation, premium product design, strong branding and retail partnerships. | Competitive advantages influence profitability and future performance. |

---

### Initial Assessment

Breville is not simply a designer, developer, and distributor.

It is a premium consumer products company that competes through:

- Innovation
- Brand strength
- Product quality
- Distribution
- Customer experience

Understanding these characteristics is the first step in building a meaningful financial model.

---

## Step 2 — Identify the Business Drivers

Every financial model begins with identifying the variables that drive performance.

Using the annual report, identify the key drivers of Breville's business.

| Driver | Why It Matters |
|---------|----------------|
| Revenue Growth | Expands the overall size of the business. |
| Product Mix | Premium products generally support higher margins. |
| Gross Margin | Indicates pricing power and manufacturing efficiency. |
| Operating Expenses | Reflect the cost of growing the business. |
| Capital Expenditure | Supports future innovation and expansion. |
| Working Capital | Inventory and receivables determine how efficiently cash is managed. |

These assumptions will ultimately drive every forecast in the financial model.

---

## Step 3 — Read Breville's Financial Statements Like an Analyst

Now that you understand Breville's business model, it's time to connect that understanding to the numbers.

As you read each financial statement, don't just ask **"What changed?"**

Instead ask:

> **"Why did it change?"**

Every number in the financial statements tells part of Breville's story.

---

### Income Statement — How Does Breville Make Money?

Start with the Income Statement.

As you read through it, look for evidence of Breville's business strategy.

| Question | What to Look For |
|----------|------------------|
| Is revenue growing? | Has Breville successfully expanded into new markets or launched successful products? |
| How stable are gross margins? | Does premium pricing allow Breville to maintain healthy margins despite inflation or higher manufacturing costs? |
| Are operating expenses increasing? | Is the company investing more heavily in marketing, R&D or international expansion? |
| Is profitability improving? | Are investments translating into stronger EBIT and Net Profit over time? |

Rather than memorising the numbers, try to explain **why** they changed.

---

### Balance Sheet — Where Has Breville Invested?

Next, review the Balance Sheet.

Think of it as a snapshot of how management has deployed capital.

| Question | What to Look For |
|----------|------------------|
| Is inventory increasing? | Is Breville preparing for future demand, or could inventory be building too quickly? |
| Are receivables growing faster than sales? | Could customers be taking longer to pay? |
| Has cash increased? | Is the company generating more cash than it needs for operations? |
| Are borrowings increasing? | Is growth being financed through debt or internally generated cash? |
| Is shareholders' equity increasing? | Is the business creating value over time? |

The Balance Sheet tells you how management is funding future growth.

---

### Cash Flow Statement — Does Breville Turn Profit Into Cash?

Finally, analyse the Cash Flow Statement.

Remember:

> **Revenue is important. Profit is better. Cash is essential.**

Focus on the following questions.

| Question | Why It Matters |
|----------|----------------|
| Is operating cash flow consistently positive? | Strong operating cash flow indicates the underlying business is healthy. |
| How much cash is reinvested into the business? | Capital expenditure reveals management's commitment to future growth. |
| Does Breville generate free cash flow? | Free cash flow ultimately drives company valuation. |
| How is excess cash being used? | Debt repayment, dividends, acquisitions or share buybacks all tell different stories about management's priorities. |

A company can report strong accounting profits while generating weak cash flow.

Professional analysts always reconcile the two.

---

## Putting the Three Statements Together

Now connect the dots.

Suppose Breville launches a successful new espresso machine.

How would that flow through the financial statements?

| Business Event | Financial Impact |
|----------------|------------------|
| Strong product sales | Revenue increases on the Income Statement. |
| Higher manufacturing volumes | Cost of Sales increases, but operating leverage may improve margins. |
| Higher Net Profit | Retained Earnings increase on the Balance Sheet. |
| Increased customer demand | Inventory may temporarily decline before being replenished. |
| Customers pay for purchases | Operating Cash Flow increases and Cash on the Balance Sheet grows. |

This exercise illustrates an important principle:

> **Financial statements are not independent reports—they are different perspectives on the same business.**

As an analyst, your job is to understand the commercial events that connect them.

Before building your financial model, ask yourself:

- What are Breville's strongest financial trends?
- Which metrics concern you most?
- What assumptions would you make if forecasting the next five years?

These observations will become the foundation of every projection you build.

---

## Step 4 — Follow the Story Through the Statements

One of the most valuable exercises is tracing how one business event flows through all three financial statements.

For example:

| Business Event | Financial Impact |
|----------------|------------------|
| Higher product sales | Revenue increases on the Income Statement. |
| Higher profits | Net Income increases. |
| Higher retained earnings | Equity increases on the Balance Sheet. |
| Customer payments received | Cash increases on the Cash Flow Statement and Balance Sheet. |

This interconnectedness is the foundation of every professional financial model.

---

## Step 5 — Build Forecast Assumptions

Financial models are not built by predicting the future.

They are built by making informed assumptions.

Using the annual report, consider:

| Question | Example Considerations |
|-----------|------------------------|
| Can revenue continue growing? | International expansion, new product launches and consumer demand. |
| Can margins improve? | Product mix, pricing power and operational efficiency. |
| Will operating expenses grow? | Investment in people, marketing and innovation. |
| How much capital investment is required? | Manufacturing, technology and product development. |

Every forecast should have a logical business explanation.

---

## Step 6 — Validate the Model

Before relying on any model, ensure it behaves as expected.

Ask yourself:

- Do the three financial statements remain linked?
- Does the Balance Sheet balance?
- Are cash flows reasonable?
- Do forecast trends align with management commentary?
- Are assumptions internally consistent?

A model that balances but produces unrealistic forecasts is still a poor model.

Validation requires both technical accuracy and commercial judgement.

---

## Step 7 — What Does the Model Tell Us?

Once the model is complete, it becomes more than a forecasting tool.

It becomes a framework for asking better questions.

For example:

- What happens if revenue growth slows?
- How sensitive are profits to gross margin changes?
- Can the company fund future expansion internally?
- Is working capital becoming more efficient?
- How resilient is the business during an economic slowdown?

These are the questions that finance professionals answer every day.

## Why This Matters

Many beginners think financial modelling is about Excel.

Professional analysts know otherwise.

A financial model is a structured representation of how a business works.

Excel is simply the tool used to communicate that understanding.

If you understand the business well, building the spreadsheet becomes relatively straightforward.

If you do not understand the business, even the most sophisticated spreadsheet will produce unreliable results.

---

## Takeaway

The purpose of a Three-Statement Financial Model is not to predict the future perfectly.

Its purpose is to create a structured framework for understanding how decisions, assumptions and business performance affect a company's financial outcomes.

Every valuation model—DCF, LBO and Merger Model—begins with a well-constructed three-statement financial model.

Without it, every subsequent analysis rests on an unstable foundation.

> **Build the business first. Everything else follows.**
