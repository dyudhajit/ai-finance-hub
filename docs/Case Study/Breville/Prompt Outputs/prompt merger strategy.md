# AI Walkthrough - Merger Strategic Analysis

In this exercise, we'll step into the shoes of a banker advising **De'Longhi S.p.A.**, one of Breville's largest global competitors.

The objective is not to build a merger model immediately.

Instead, begin by answering the question every deal team asks before opening Excel:

> **Should De'Longhi acquire Breville?**

---

## Step 1 — Strategic Rationale

Read the Breville FY2025 Annual Report.

Imagine you are preparing a discussion paper for De'Longhi's Board.

Consider the following observations.

| Question | Breville Observation | Potential Value to De'Longhi |
|-----------|----------------------|------------------------------|
| Does Breville own strong brands? | Breville has built premium kitchen appliance brands with strong consumer recognition across Australia, North America and Europe. | Expands De'Longhi's premium brand portfolio while strengthening its position in higher-margin kitchen appliances. |
| Does Breville operate in attractive markets? | North America contributes the majority of revenue and continues to be Breville's largest growth market. | Accelerates De'Longhi's expansion in the US without building distribution organically. |
| Does Breville have differentiated products? | Continuous investment in product innovation and premium consumer experiences. | Provides complementary products and strengthens innovation capabilities. |
| Does Breville possess attractive distribution? | Extensive retail partnerships and growing direct-to-consumer capabilities. | Immediate access to established distribution channels and customer relationships. |

---

### Initial Assessment

From a strategic perspective, the acquisition appears attractive because it would allow De'Longhi to:

- Strengthen its premium appliance portfolio.
- Increase exposure to North America.
- Expand innovation capabilities.
- Increase scale in small domestic appliances.

Notice that this conclusion comes from understanding the business—not from building a financial model.

---

## Step 2 — Revenue Synergies

Now ask:

> **How could the combined company generate more revenue than the two businesses separately?**

Potential revenue synergies include:

| Opportunity | Example |
|-------------|----------|
| Cross-selling | Selling Breville espresso machines through De'Longhi's established European distribution network. |
| Distribution | Introducing De'Longhi products into Breville's strongest North American retail relationships. |
| Geographic Expansion | Accelerating Breville's expansion into markets where De'Longhi already has a strong presence. |
| Customer Base | Leveraging combined customer loyalty programmes and direct-to-consumer channels. |
| Product Innovation | Combining engineering and product development teams to launch new premium products faster. |

Revenue synergies are often difficult to quantify but can become significant over the long term.

---

## Step 3 — Cost Synergies

Next, identify where operating costs could be reduced.

Examples include:

| Function | Potential Synergy |
|----------|-------------------|
| Procurement | Increased purchasing power with suppliers. |
| Manufacturing | Rationalising production facilities and improving capacity utilisation. |
| Logistics | Consolidating warehouses and freight networks. |
| Corporate Functions | Combining Finance, HR, IT and Legal teams. |
| Marketing | Shared global campaigns and reduced duplication. |

Unlike revenue synergies, cost synergies are generally easier to estimate and are often the primary source of value creation in acquisitions.

---

## Step 4 — Integration Challenges

Every acquisition introduces execution risk.

Potential integration challenges include:

| Challenge | Why It Matters |
|-----------|----------------|
| Culture | Both companies have different management styles and corporate cultures. |
| Brand Positioning | Maintaining the premium identity of both brands while avoiding customer confusion. |
| Technology | Integrating ERP systems, reporting processes and data infrastructure. |
| Supply Chain | Aligning manufacturing locations, suppliers and inventory management. |
| Product Overlap | Rationalising overlapping product lines without losing customers. |

The quality of integration often determines whether expected synergies are realised.

---

## Step 5 — Risks That Could Destroy Value

Even strategically attractive acquisitions can fail.

Potential risks include:

| Risk | Potential Impact |
|------|------------------|
| Paying too much | Future returns fail to justify the acquisition premium. |
| Synergies not realised | Expected cost savings never materialise. |
| Integration delays | Operational disruption reduces profitability. |
| Customer attrition | Existing customers switch to competitors during integration. |
| Management distraction | Leadership focus shifts from operating the business to integrating the acquisition. |

Investment bankers spend significant time evaluating these risks before recommending a transaction.

---

## Preliminary Investment View

Based solely on the qualitative analysis of the annual report, the acquisition appears strategically compelling.

**Strengths**

- Highly complementary product portfolios.
- Strong geographic fit.
- Opportunity to expand distribution.
- Significant procurement and operating scale benefits.
- Attractive premium consumer brands.

**Key Risks**

- Execution of integration.
- Maintaining both brand identities.
- Achieving projected synergies.
- Paying an appropriate acquisition premium.

At this stage, the recommendation is **not**:

> "De'Longhi should acquire Breville."

Instead, the recommendation is:

> **"The transaction appears strategically attractive and warrants detailed financial analysis."**

This is precisely where merger modelling begins.

The next step will require determining whether the strategic rationale is supported by the financial evidence.
